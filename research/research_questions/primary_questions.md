## Research Question 1: Temporal and Regional Variations

### Question Statement

**How does electricity usage vary by region, time of day, and season, and what factors drive these variations?**

### Theoretical Foundation

This question is grounded in multiple theoretical frameworks:

1. **Energy Economics Theory** (Kaya Identity, Kuznets Curve)
   - Energy consumption = Population × GDP per capita × Energy intensity
   - Economic development drives consumption patterns
   - Regional economic differences explain consumption variations

2. **Geographic Analysis Theory** (Spatial Analysis)
   - Spatial autocorrelation: Nearby regions have similar characteristics
   - Scale effects: Patterns vary by geographic scale
   - Context matters: Local factors (climate, economy) affect consumption

3. **Time Series Theory** (Seasonality, Trends)
   - Temporal patterns: Daily, weekly, seasonal cycles
   - Trend analysis: Long-term changes in consumption
   - Autocorrelation: Current consumption depends on past consumption

4. **Behavioral Economics** (Consumer Behavior)
   - Daily routines drive hourly patterns
   - Lifestyle differences affect consumption
   - Seasonal behavior changes (heating, cooling needs)

### Context and Background

**Why This Question Matters:**
- Understanding temporal patterns enables demand forecasting
- Regional variations identify areas needing different strategies
- Peak identification helps plan capacity requirements
- Seasonal patterns inform infrastructure investment timing

**Literature Support:**
- [Cite papers on temporal patterns in electricity demand]
- [Cite papers on regional energy consumption variations]
- [Cite papers on factors driving consumption differences]

### Sub-Questions

1. **Temporal Patterns**:
   - What are the daily consumption patterns (hourly variations)?
   - How does consumption differ between weekdays and weekends?
   - What are the seasonal patterns (summer vs winter peaks)?
   - Are there long-term trends in consumption?

2. **Regional Variations**:
   - Which regions have the highest consumption?
   - How do regional consumption patterns differ?
   - What factors explain regional differences (economy, climate, population)?

3. **Interaction Effects**:
   - How do temporal patterns vary by region?
   - Do seasonal patterns differ by region?
   - Are there region-specific peak periods?

### Objectives

1. **Quantify Patterns**: Measure and quantify temporal and regional variations
2. **Identify Drivers**: Determine factors driving these variations
3. **Understand Interactions**: Explore how temporal and regional patterns interact
4. **Inform Planning**: Use patterns to inform infrastructure planning

### Methodology

1. **Temporal Analysis**: Time series decomposition, statistical tests for seasonality
2. **Regional Analysis**: Geographic clustering, comparative analysis
3. **Correlation Analysis**: Identify correlations with economic, demographic, climate factors
4. **Visualization**: Create interactive visualizations of patterns

### Expected Outcomes

1. **Quantified Patterns**: Statistical measures of temporal and regional variations
2. **Driver Identification**: List of factors explaining variations
3. **Pattern Maps**: Visualizations showing patterns across time and space
4. **Insights**: Understanding of how patterns inform infrastructure planning

### Contribution to Research

This question provides the foundation for:
- Understanding current consumption patterns
- Identifying factors affecting demand
- Informing forecasting models
- Supporting risk assessment

## Research Question 2: Demand Spikes and Grid Stress

### Question Statement

**Are there recurring demand spikes that could stress the grid, and what factors contribute to these spikes?**

### Theoretical Foundation

1. **Grid Reliability Theory** (NERC Standards)
   - Reserve margin: Available capacity above peak demand
   - Reliability standards: Minimum reserve margins for grid stability
   - Peak demand: Maximum demand that grid must serve

2. **Risk Assessment Theory** (Extreme Events)
   - Extreme value theory: Analyzing rare but critical events
   - Stress testing: Identifying system vulnerabilities
   - Threshold analysis: Identifying critical demand levels

3. **Event Analysis** (Causal Inference)
   - Identifying triggers for demand spikes
   - Understanding event-driven consumption
   - Predicting future spike events

### Context and Background

**Why This Question Matters:**
- Demand spikes can cause blackouts and brownouts
- Understanding spikes enables proactive capacity planning
- Identifying spike patterns helps develop demand response programs
- Extreme weather events increasingly stress grids

**Literature Support:**
- [Cite papers on grid reliability and reserve margins]
- [Cite papers on demand spikes and extreme events]
- [Cite papers on weather impact on electricity demand]

### Sub-Questions

1. **Spike Identification**:
   - When do demand spikes occur?
   - How frequent are spikes?
   - What is the magnitude of spikes?
   - Which regions experience most spikes?

2. **Spike Causes**:
   - What factors cause demand spikes?
   - Are spikes weather-related?
   - Do spikes correlate with special events?
   - Are there predictable patterns?

3. **Grid Impact**:
   - Which regions have low reserve margins during spikes?
   - What is the risk of grid failure during spikes?
   - Are there transmission bottlenecks during spikes?

### Objectives

1. **Identify Spikes**: Systematically identify demand spikes in historical data
2. **Understand Causes**: Determine factors contributing to spikes
3. **Assess Risk**: Evaluate grid stress during spike events
4. **Predict Future Spikes**: Develop methods to predict future spike events

### Methodology

1. **Spike Detection**: Statistical methods (Z-score, IQR) to identify outliers
2. **Event Analysis**: Correlate spikes with weather, events, economic factors
3. **Risk Assessment**: Calculate reserve margins during spike periods
4. **Pattern Recognition**: Identify recurring patterns in spike occurrence

### Expected Outcomes

1. **Spike Catalog**: Database of identified demand spikes with characteristics
2. **Causal Analysis**: Understanding of factors contributing to spikes
3. **Risk Map**: Visualization of regions vulnerable to spike-related stress
4. **Prediction Framework**: Methods for predicting future spikes

### Contribution to Research

This question:
- Identifies critical stress points in the grid
- Enables proactive capacity planning
- Supports demand response program development
- Informs emergency planning

## Research Question 4: Long-Term Forecasting

### Question Statement

**Can we accurately forecast electricity demand for the next 5–10 years in specific regions using time series and machine learning methods, and what factors affect forecast accuracy?**

### Theoretical Foundation

1. **Time Series Theory** (ARIMA, SARIMA)
   - Stationarity: Some processes are stationary
   - Trend and seasonality: Decomposable components
   - Autocorrelation: Dependence on past values

2. **Machine Learning Theory** (Supervised Learning)
   - Ensemble methods: Combining multiple models
   - Feature engineering: Extracting meaningful features
   - Cross-validation: Validating models on unseen data

3. **Forecasting Theory** (Prediction Intervals)
   - Uncertainty quantification: Forecasts are probabilistic
   - Long-term forecasting: Challenges of long horizons
   - Model selection: Choosing appropriate models

### Context and Background

**Why This Question Matters:**
- Infrastructure planning requires long-term forecasts
- Accurate forecasts enable proactive investment
- Understanding forecast accuracy helps decision-making
- Long-term forecasts support strategic planning

**Literature Support:**
- [Cite papers on electricity demand forecasting]
- [Cite papers on time series forecasting methods]
- [Cite papers on machine learning for forecasting]

### Sub-Questions

1. **Method Comparison**:
   - Which forecasting methods work best?
   - Do ensemble methods outperform individual models?
   - How do methods compare across regions?

2. **Accuracy Assessment**:
   - What accuracy can we achieve for 5-10 year forecasts?
   - How does accuracy vary by region?
   - What factors affect forecast accuracy?

3. **Uncertainty Quantification**:
   - How do we quantify forecast uncertainty?
   - What are confidence intervals for forecasts?
   - How does uncertainty increase with forecast horizon?

### Objectives

1. **Develop Models**: Build forecasting models using multiple methods
2. **Validate Accuracy**: Test models on historical data
3. **Generate Forecasts**: Produce 5-10 year regional forecasts
4. **Assess Uncertainty**: Quantify forecast uncertainty

### Methodology

1. **Model Development**: ARIMA, Prophet, XGBoost, Ensemble
2. **Model Validation**: Train/test split, cross-validation, backtesting

4. **Accuracy Assessment**: RMSE, MAE, MAPE metrics

### Expected Outcomes

1. **Forecast Models**: Validated models for regional forecasting
2. **Accuracy Metrics**: Quantified forecast accuracy
3. **Regional Forecasts**: 5-10 year forecasts for each region
4. **Uncertainty Estimates**: Confidence intervals for forecasts

- Validates forecasting methods

## Research Question 5: Risk Assessment and Under-Supply

### Question Statement

**Which areas are at risk of under-supply if current trends continue, what are the root causes of this risk, and what are the timelines for potential shortages?**

### Theoretical Foundation

1. **Risk Assessment Theory** (Quantitative Risk Analysis)
   - Risk = Probability × Impact
   - Reserve margin: Key indicator of supply adequacy
   - Scenario analysis: Considering multiple futures

2. **Grid Reliability Theory** (NERC Standards)
   - Minimum reserve margins: Standards for grid reliability
   - Reliability assessment: Systematic evaluation of supply adequacy
   - Contingency planning: Preparing for supply shortfalls

3. **Causal Analysis** (Root Cause Analysis)
   - Identifying primary causes of risk
   - Understanding causal chains
   - Prioritizing interventions

### Context and Background

**Why This Question Matters:**
- Identifying at-risk regions enables proactive investment
- Understanding root causes enables targeted solutions
- Knowing timelines helps prioritize investments
- Preventing shortages is more cost-effective than responding

**Literature Support:**
- [Cite papers on grid reliability and reserve margins]
- [Cite papers on supply risk assessment]
- [Cite papers on infrastructure investment planning]

### Sub-Questions

1. **Risk Identification**:
   - Which regions have low reserve margins?
   - What regions have high demand growth?
   - Which regions are at risk of under-supply?

2. **Root Cause Analysis**:
   - What are the primary causes of supply risk?

   - Are there infrastructure bottlenecks?

3. **Timeline Assessment**:

### Objectives

### Expected Outcomes

- Supports evidence-based decision-making
